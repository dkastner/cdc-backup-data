Top Stories

Let’s Prevent Cancer Together: January is Cervical Health Awareness Month, and we need your help to spread the word that HPV vaccination is cancer prevention. Every year, about 4,000 women die from cervical cancer in the U.S.—even with screening and treatment. Many more women undergo invasive testing and treatment for cervical cancers and precancers that can have lasting effects. Help get the word out about the benefits of HPV vaccination by joining the #PreventCancerTogether Thunderclap page. Thunderclap is a social media tool that allows supporters to sign up in advance to share a unified message at a specific time via their individual Facebook or Twitter accounts. On January 19, Thunderclap will release the message on the timelines of all those that signed up, thus creating a social media Thunderclap of support. Lend your social support by signing up now.

You can also talk about HPV vaccination throughout January by linking to our matte articles and HPV vaccination videos, or by sharing a powerful podcast from a cervical cancer survivor on your website. You can also provide the link to CDC’s updated HPV website, which features a train the trainer PowerPoint to clinicians in your community.

Vaccine Storage, Handling, and Administration: The December 18 Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report (MMWR) includes a report on a recent incident of injection safety, vaccine administration, and storage and handling errors at an employee flu vaccination clinic in New Jersey.

CDC continues to promote safe injection and vaccine management practices and encourages our public health partners to do the same. Available below are updated guidance for vaccination clinics and useful web resources for immunization service providers and businesses planning vaccination clinics.

  • December 18 MMWR. “Notes from the Field—Injection Safety and Vaccine Administration Errors at an Employee Influenza Vaccination Clinic in New Jersey, 2015.” This article details the vaccine administration and vaccine storage and handling errors committed by a health services company contracted to provide an employee flu vaccination clinic, and how the state immunization program responded to the situation.
  • Guidelines for Large-scale Influenza Vaccination Clinic Planning. This site provides guidelines and recommendations to assist with planning influenza vaccination clinics. Topics include clinic logistics, as well as vaccine storage, handling, and administration.
  • At-A-Glance Resource Guide—Vaccine Administration and Storage and Handling [1 page]. A quick guide to key web resources on immunization, vaccine administration, and vaccine storage and handling. It includes CDC guidelines, an immunization checklist, educational webinars, and standing orders.

For more information about these and other CDC resources, contact Alex Shevach or Julie Zier.

Influenza Information

New Influenza Vaccination Recommendations Training Video: CDC has recently released a new web-on-demand video titled “New Influenza Vaccination Recommendations.” This training video addresses the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) influenza vaccine recommendations, the benefits of influenza vaccination, best practices in influenza vaccine storage, handling, and administration, and various other topics. It is intended for health care providers and Continuing Education (CE) credit is available. Health care providers should remain up to date with their knowledge of influenza vaccination and practices.

Influenza Activity Update in the U.S. from October 4–November 28, 2015: CDC collects, compiles, and analyzes data on influenza activity year-round in the U.S. The influenza season generally begins in the fall and continues through the winter and spring months; however, the timing and severity of circulating influenza viruses can vary by geographic location and season. Influenza activity in the U.S. remained low through October and November in 2015. Influenza A viruses have been most frequently identified, with influenza A (H3) viruses predominating. The December 11 MMWR summarizes U.S. influenza activity for the period October 4–November 28, 2015.

Thank You for Participating in National Influenza Vaccination Week 2015:

National Influenza Vaccination Week (NIVW) was held Dec 6–12, 2015. An annual national observance of this scope requires high-quality collaboration and participation. As we’ve said many times, the success of NIVW relies on the involvement of our partners at all levels. Some highlights from the week include:

  • A CDC Thunderclap social media campaign message that was shared on December 7. We exceeded our goal of 100 supporters by reaching 201! This campaign generated more than 5 million impressions across Facebook and Twitter platforms.
  • A CDC-hosted Twitter chat featuring CDC influenza subject matter expert, Dr. Grace Appiah. This hour-long chat generated an estimated 38.5 million impressions, with 693 participants using the #NIVW2015 hashtag.
  • Two infographics were updated, one that showed the benefits of vaccination [1 page] for the 2014–15 flu season, and another reminding pregnant women [1 page] to get vaccinated.
  • CDC also posted national early-season vaccination coverage estimates. Despite the benefits of flu vaccination, fewer than half of the people in the U.S. had reported getting a flu vaccine this season as of early November 2015. National early-season vaccination coverage estimates indicate that 39% of Americans 6 months and older had received a flu vaccination by early November 2015 (39% of adults and 39.2% of children). This is similar to flu vaccination coverage last season at the same time. Other online coverage reports indicated that vaccination among pregnant women (40.2%) and health care providers (66.7%) is about the same as it was this time last year. Among health care personnel, the agency again noted the highest vaccination rates among physicians (87.5%), nurse practitioners/physician assistants (81.8%), pharmacists (76.8%), and other clinical professionals (72.6%). Flu vaccination was lowest among administrative and non-clinical support staff (62.8%) and assistants and aides (55.4%).

Thank you to all of our partners for contributing to the success of NIVW 2015!

Continuing Flu Vaccination Efforts for the 2015–16 Flu Season: Flu activity is low so far this season, but increases in activity are expected in the coming weeks. Flu activity most often peaks in February and can last into May. As long as flu viruses are circulating, it is not too late to get a flu vaccination, even in January or later. Remember, it takes about two weeks after vaccination for the body to build up antibodies to protect against the flu, making now the perfect time to be vaccinated.

A variety of resources are still available. They include print materials, videos, PSAs, mobile content, toolkits, and web tools.

Check out our interactive #VaxWithMe selfie campaign. This social media campaign encourages individuals to share photos and videos of themselves during or after getting their flu vaccination, using the hashtag #VaxWithMe. Selfies can be shared on Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, and YouTube social media platforms.

Flu-related questions and information requests (including requests for routine e-mail updates) should be directed to CDC at fluinbox@cdc.gov.

Resources and Information

Epidemiology and Prevention of Vaccine-Preventable Diseases, 13th Edition (the Pink Book): CDC, NCIRD, and the Public Health Foundation (PHF) are pleased to announce that the book, Epidemiology and Prevention of Vaccine-Preventable Diseases, 13th Edition is available. The Pink Book provides public health and health care professionals with the most comprehensive and up-to-date information on vaccines and vaccine-preventable diseases. The Pink Book is available for purchase from the PHF Learning Resource Center, and the chapters and appendices can be viewed/downloaded from the NCIRD vaccines site.

Webinar Series for Pink Book Recasts: Recasts of this webinar series, which presented summaries of sections from the 13th edition of the Pink Book, are available online. Each of the 15 webinar recasts in the series is approximately one hour in length. Continuing Education (CE) credit has expired for the webinars. Please visit the webinar series web page for the recasts and additional information.

Current Issues in Immunization Netconferences: Immunization netconferences are live, one-hour presentations combining an online visual presentation with simultaneous audio via telephone conference call and a live question and answer session. Internet access and a separate phone line are needed to participate. The next netconference will be held on March 16, 2016. Please visit the netconference web page for additional information and archived webcasts.

You Call the Shots Modules: You Call the Shots is a web-based training course developed through the Project to Enhance Immunization Content in Nursing Education and Training. Several updated modules are now available, including Tetanus, Diphtheria, and Pertussis (Tdap), Diphtheria, Tetanus, and Pertussis (DTaP), and Influenza. Please visit the You Call the Shots web page for additional information and other modules. Continuing Education (CE) credit is available for viewing a module and completing an evaluation.

ACIP Meeting: The next ACIP meeting will be held February 24–25, 2016. Please visit the ACIP meeting web page for agendas, presentation slides, meeting minutes, archived video broadcasts, and additional information.

Measles Resources: CDC aims to continue increasing awareness of measles among individuals and families and to encourage MMR vaccination. To support disease prevention and vaccination education efforts, CDC has developed a variety of measles and vaccination resources, including fact sheets, podcasts, and matte articles. There are also infographics available in English and Spanish.

CDC and Medscape: This special series of commentaries, part of a collaboration between CDC and Medscape, is designed to deliver CDC’s authoritative guidance directly to Medscape’s physicians, nurses, pharmacists, and other health care providers. In this series, CDC experts offer video commentaries on current topics important to practicing clinicians. NCIRD has contributed to a variety of commentaries. You may need to sign up and log in as a member to view the commentaries. Registration is free.

Immunization Resources: Publications are available for ordering at CDC-INFO on Demand. You can search for immunization publications by using the “Programs” drop-down menu and selecting “Immunization and Vaccines,” or you can search by “Title.” Numerous items are available, including the 2015 immunization schedules, the updated Parents’ Guide to Childhood Immunizations, and various campaign materials.

CDC Job Openings: CDC is committed to recruiting and hiring qualified candidates for a wide range of immunization and other positions. Researchers, medical officers, epidemiologists, and other specialists are often needed to fill positions within CDC. For a current listing, including international opportunities, please visit CDC’s employment web page.
