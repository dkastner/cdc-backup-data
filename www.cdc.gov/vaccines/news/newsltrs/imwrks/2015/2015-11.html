Top Stories

Progress Toward Regional Measles Elimination—Worldwide, 2000–2014: In 2000, the United Nations General Assembly adopted the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs), one of which was a two-thirds reduction in child mortality by 2015, with measles vaccination coverage as one of the three indicators of progress toward this goal. In 2010, the World Health Assembly established three milestones for measles control by 2015: 1) increase routine coverage with the first dose of measles-containing vaccine (MCV1) for children aged 1 year to more than 90% nationally and more than 80% in every district; 2) reduce global annual measles incidence to fewer than five cases per million population; and 3) reduce global measles mortality by 95% from the 2000 estimate. In 2012, the World Health Assembly endorsed the Global Vaccine Action Plan, with the objective of eliminating measles in four World Health Organization (WHO) regions by 2015. WHO member states in all six WHO regions have adopted measles elimination goals. The November 13 MMWR updates the 2000–2013 report and describes progress toward global control and regional measles elimination during 2000–2014. During this period, annual reported measles incidence declined 73% worldwide, from 146 to 40 cases per million population, and annual estimated measles deaths declined 79%, from 546,800 to 114,900. However, progress toward the 2015 milestones and elimination goals has slowed markedly since 2010. To resume progress toward milestones and goals for measles elimination, a review of current strategies and challenges to improving program performance is needed, and countries and their partners need to raise awareness of measles elimination goals, address barriers to measles vaccination, and make substantial and sustained additional investments in strengthening health systems.

Global Routine Vaccination Coverage: 2014 marked the 40th anniversary of the WHO Expanded Program on Immunization, which was established to ensure equitable access to routine immunization services. Since 1974, global coverage with the four core vaccines (Bacille Calmette-Guérin [BCG] vaccine, which protect against tuberculosis, diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis [DTP] vaccine, poliovirus vaccine, and measles vaccine) has increased from less than 5% to more than 85%, and additional vaccines have been added to the recommended schedule. Coverage with the third dose of DTP vaccine (DTP3) by age 12 months is an indicator of immunization program performance because it reflects completion of the basic infant immunization schedule. Coverage with other vaccines, including the third dose of poliovirus vaccine (polio3) and the first dose of measles-containing vaccine (MCV1), is also assessed. Estimated global DTP3 coverage has remained at 84%–86% since 2009, with estimated 2014 coverage at 86%. Estimated global coverage for the second routine dose of measles-containing vaccine (MCV2) was 38% by age 24 months and 56% when older age groups were included, similar to levels reported in 2013 (36% and 55%, respectively). Adequate vaccine stock management and additional opportunities for immunization (such as through routine visits in the second year of life) are integral components for strengthening immunization programs, reaching and sustaining high immunization coverage in all countries, and reducing morbidity and mortality from vaccine-preventable diseases. Please read the November 13 MMWR for the full report.

Human Papillomavirus Vaccination Coverage among Female Adolescents in Managed Care Plans in the U.S. During 2013: Human papillomavirus (HPV) is the most common sexually transmitted infection, with a reported 79 million persons aged 15–59 years currently infected in the U.S., and approximately 14 million new cases diagnosed each year. Although most HPV infections are asymptomatic and transient and do not cause disease, persistent HPV infection can lead to cervical, vulvar, vaginal, anal, penile, and oropharyngeal cancer. In the U.S., approximately 27,000 cancer cases are attributed to HPV each year. HPV vaccination, which is routinely recommended for adolescents aged 11–12 years, is an effective primary prevention strategy that can reduce many of the HPV infections that lead to cancer. To determine whether the recommended HPV vaccination series is currently being administered to adolescents with health insurance, CDC and the National Committee for Quality Assurance (NCQA) assessed 2013 data from the Health Care Effectiveness Data and Information Set (HEDIS). The HEDIS HPV Vaccine for Female Adolescents performance measure evaluates the proportion of female adolescent members in commercial and Medicaid health plans who receive the recommended 3-dose HPV vaccination series by age 13 years. In 2013, the median HPV vaccination coverage levels for U.S. female adolescents among commercial and Medicaid plans were 12% and 19%, respectively (ranges=0%–34% for commercial plans; 5%–52% for Medicaid plans). Improving HPV vaccination coverage and understanding what health plans might do to support HPV vaccination are needed, including understanding the barriers to, and facilitators for, vaccination coverage. Please read the October 30 MMWR for the full report.

Progress Toward Poliomyelitis Eradication in Pakistan, January 2014–September 2015: Since Nigeria reported its last case of wild poliovirus type 1 (WPV1) in July 2014, Pakistan and Afghanistan remain the only two countries where WPV transmission has never been interrupted. The November 20 MMWR describes actions taken and progress achieved toward polio eradication in Pakistan during January 2014–September 2015 and updates previous reports. A total of 38 WPV1 cases were reported in Pakistan during January–September 2015, compared with 243 during the same period in 2014. Focused efforts to close immunity gaps by locating, tracking, and vaccinating continually missed children and improving coverage with OPV through the routine vaccination program are needed to stop WPV transmission in Pakistan.

Updated Vaccine Information Statements (VISs): CDC has released updated versions of the Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine (PCV13) VIS [2 pages] and the Pediatric Multi-vaccine VIS [3 pages]. These VISs were updated from “interim” to “final” versions with minimal changes. The title of the pediatric multi-vaccine VIS was changed from “Your Baby’s First Vaccines” to “Your Child’s First Vaccines,” and “PCV13” was added to the title of the pneumococcal conjugate vaccine VIS. The formatting and some section titles also changed. CDC encourages providers to begin using these VISs immediately; however, stocks of the previous editions may be used until gone.

Influenza Information

National Influenza Vaccination Week: This year, National Influenza Vaccination Week (NIVW) will be observed December 6–12.

NIVW is intended to educate the public about the importance of the following:

  • Promoting vaccination throughout the flu season, beginning before the holidays and extending into January and beyond.
  • Highlighting the importance of universal flu vaccination for everyone age 6 months and older, as well as reminding parents about the need for a second dose for certain children younger than 9 years old.
  • Putting special focus on people at high risk for complications from flu, particularly those with asthma, diabetes, and heart and lung conditions, as well as young children, seniors, and pregnant women.
  • Addressing disparities in vaccination coverage by developing and sustaining partnerships with grassroots organizations and trusted messengers.

Please check the NIVW web page for additional information, including CDC plans, materials, messages and web tools.

Upcoming NIVW Events:

  • December 7: #NIVW2015 Thunderclap Campaign
  • December 8: CDC-hosted Twitter Chat, featuring subject matter expert Dr. Grace Appiah (hashtag #NIVW2015)
  • December 11 and 14: Radio Media Tour featuring various CDC subject matter experts

We look forward to your participation!

CDC has launched the #VaxWithMe selfie campaign as an innovative way to capture and share flu vaccination promotion activities posted with the #VaxWithMe hashtag across various digital platforms (Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, and YouTube). To get involved, just post a selfie getting your flu vaccine and use the hashtag #VaxWithMe. CDC posts weekly updates to the interactive campaign timeline.

Resources and Information

Epidemiology and Prevention of Vaccine-Preventable Diseases, 13th Edition (the Pink Book): CDC, NCIRD, and the Public Health Foundation (PHF) are pleased to announce the availability of the book, Epidemiology and Prevention of Vaccine-Preventable Diseases, 13th Edition. The Pink Book provides public health and health care professionals with the most comprehensive and up-to-date information on vaccines and vaccine-preventable diseases. The Pink Book is available for purchase from the PHF Learning Resource Center, and the chapters and appendices can be viewed/downloaded from the NCIRD vaccines site.

Webinar Series for Pink Book: Recasts of this webinar series, which presented summaries of sections from the 13th edition of the Pink Book, are now available online. Each of the 15 webinar recasts in the series is approximately one hour in length. Continuing Education (CE) credit has expired for the webinars. Please visit the webinar series web page for the recasts and additional information.

Vaccine Storage, Handling, and Administration: CDC encourages best practices in vaccine storage, handling, and administration, and has compiled best practices guidance for storing and handling vaccine, and using proper administration techniques. Health care professionals who are responsible for storing, handling, and/or administering vaccine can review the guidance online to help ensure that their patients are receiving safe and effective vaccines.

The Vaccine Storage and Handling toolkit [4.3 MB, 109 pages] provides comprehensive guidance for health care professionals on vaccine storage and handling recommendations and best practice strategies. It includes equipment considerations (including storage units and temperature monitoring devices), cold chain maintenance strategies, routine storage and handling practices, inventory management, and emergency procedures for protecting vaccine inventories. Additional vaccine storage and handling resources are available on the vaccine storage and handling web page.

The Pink Book contains a “Vaccine Administration” chapter, which includes guidance for staff training and education, patient care before and during vaccine administration, infection control, vaccine preparation, correct route and site, as well as other topics. Other vaccine recommendations are also available on the Pink Book web page.

Current Issues in Immunization Netconferences: Immunization netconferences are live, one-hour presentations combining an online visual presentation with simultaneous audio via telephone conference call and a live question and answer session. Internet access and a separate phone line are needed to participate. The latest netconference was held on October 28, 2015. Candice Robinson was the moderator and the topics were “Vaccination of Adults with Altered Immunocompetence,” presented by Andrew Kroger, and updates on intervals between PCV13 and PPSV23 vaccines, presented by Miwako Koyabashi. Please visit the netconference web page for additional information and archived webcasts.

You Call the Shots Modules: You Call the Shots is a web-based training course developed through the Project to Enhance Immunization Content in Nursing Education and Training. Several updated modules are now available, including, Diphtheria, Tetanus, and Pertussis (DTaP) and Influenza. Please visit the You Call the Shots web page for additional information and other modules. Continuing Education (CE) credit is available for viewing a module and completing an evaluation.

Putting Adolescent Meningococcal Vaccine Recommendations into Practice: All 11-to 12-year-olds should be vaccinated with a single dose of quadrivalent meningococcal conjugate vaccine, with a booster dose at age 16 years. Adolescents and young adults aged 16 through 23 years may be vaccinated with a serogroup B meningococcal (MenB) vaccine, preferably at 16 through 18 years old. The number of doses varies by vaccine type and brand. Find answers to frequently asked questions about these recommendations, including detailed information about the new MenB vaccines.

Which Vaccines do People with Diabetes Need? November is American Diabetes Month and also the month in which many adults seek influenza vaccine. It provides the ideal time to perform a complete immunization assessment to ensure patients are up-to-date on the recommended vaccines. This is especially important for patients with chronic health conditions, including diabetes, as they are at increased risk for complications from certain vaccine-preventable diseases.

Diabetes can suppress the immune response, making it harder to fight infections, so people with diabetes may be at higher risk for more serious complications from an illness than people without diabetes. Influenza can raise blood glucose to dangerously high levels, putting people with diabetes at risk for serious complications. People with diabetes are also at increased risk of death from pneumonia and other infections, and have higher rates of hepatitis B than the rest of the population. Outbreaks of hepatitis B associated with blood glucose monitoring procedures have occurred among people with diabetes, so it is important to ensure that they receive all recommended vaccines, including:

  • Annual influenza vaccine
  • Pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccine
  • Hepatitis B vaccine series

In addition, all adults need:

  • Tdap vaccine
  • Zoster vaccine if they are 60 years and older

For more information on adult vaccines, visit the Adult Vaccination Resources web page.

Adolescent Immunization Communication: CDC’s adolescent immunization communication team is working on a variety of activities and has developed a number of resources to promote adolescent immunizations and educate parents and clinicians on the importance of following the recommended immunization schedule. Resources for parents and for clinicians are available online.

There are also free print materials available for ordering at CDC-INFO on Demand. Select “Immunizations and Vaccines (Teens)” from the “Programs” drop-down menu. Materials include posters and fact sheets to raise awareness and educate parents on the importance of getting the recommended vaccines for their preteens. For more information on materials and activities, or to subscribe to the “Preteen Vaccines” newsletter, e-mail preteenvaccines@cdc.gov.

Measles Resources: CDC aims to continually increase awareness of measles among individuals and families, and to encourage MMR vaccination. To support disease prevention and vaccination education efforts, CDC has developed a variety of measles and vaccination resources, including fact sheets, podcasts, and matte articles. There are also infographics available in English and Spanish.

CDC and Medscape: This special series of commentaries, part of a collaboration between CDC and Medscape, is designed to deliver CDC’s authoritative guidance directly to Medscape’s physicians, nurses, pharmacists, and other health care providers. In this series, CDC experts offer video commentaries on current topics important to practicing clinicians. NCIRD has contributed to a variety of commentaries, including a recently released commentary titled “Why Health Care Providers Must Receive a Flu Vaccine,” featuring Carolyn Bridges from NCIRD/ISD. Immunization Resources: Publications are available for ordering at CDC-INFO on Demand. You can search for immunization publications by using the “Programs” drop-down menu and selecting “Immunization and Vaccines,” or you can search by “Title.” Numerous items are available, including the 2015 immunization schedules, the updated Parents’ Guide to Childhood Immunizations, and various campaign materials.
