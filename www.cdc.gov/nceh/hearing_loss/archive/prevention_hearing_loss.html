How To Prevent Noise-Induced Hearing Loss

Recommend on Facebook Tweet ShareCompartir

  • AT PUBLIC EVENTS
  • DURING PREGNANCY
  • FOR INFANTS AND CHILDREN
  • AT WORK
  • HOW TO CHOOSE HEARING PROTECTION DEVICES

Noise-induced hearing loss is permanent, thus prevention is vital. The effect of noise on hearing depends on how loud it is (sound intensity) and how long it lasts (duration). Avoiding noisy situations is the best prevention.

You can use a sound level meter to measure sound around you. You don’t have to be a health professional to own one, either. You can find free sound level meters developed as “apps” for smartphones. These smartphone apps typically display noise levels in decibels and can even show you the risk for hearing loss. Smartphone apps must be calibrated to ensure the readings are accurate. For more information on the accuracy of these apps, visit CDC - NIOSH Science Blog – So How Accurate Are These Smartphone Sound Measurement Apps?

Even without a device to measure sound, you can tell if sound is too loud. If you or others need to shout in order to be heard or cannot understand each other even at arm’s length away, the sound is too loud.

The three general steps you can take to protect your hearing are turning the noise down, walking away, and using hearing protection. More details include the following: 

AT HOME /LEISURE TIME
  • Turn down the volume of music.
  • If listening to loud music, take listening breaks to reduce exposure.
  • Use quieter products (power tools, toys, recreational vehicles) whenever such are available. Reduce equipment noise by replacing worn, loose, or unbalanced machine parts. Keep equipment well lubricated and maintained. For such tools, visit CDC - Buy Quiet - NIOSH Workplace Safety and Health Topics.
  • Use hearing protection devices when you cannot avoid loud sounds. Hearing protection can increase your ability to hear others.
  • Make hearing protection convenient. Stash earplugs in your car or workshop, for easy access.
  • Keep children away from noisy music or noisy equipment at home.

 Top of Page

AT PUBLIC EVENTS
  • Move or stay far away from the loudest sound-producing source, such as loudspeakers or cannons at college stadiums, especially if attending with children.
  • Limit the length of time of exposure to loud sounds.
  • Pay attention to signs and information flyers warning of possible very loud noise and recommending hearing protection.
  • Bring hearing protection devices with you. Keep them in your car, pockets, or other easy to access place.

 Top of Page

DURING PREGNANCY
  • Use same precautions as specified above
  • Avoid your body touching the vibratory source of the noise

For more information on pregnancy and noise at work, visit CDC - Reproductive Health - Noise - NIOSH Workplace Safety and Health Topic.

FOR INFANTS AND CHILDREN
  • Use same precautions as specified above
  • Keep your child away from high noise levels, such as from very loud toys.

For information about hearing loss in children visit Facts | Hearing Loss | NCBDDD | CDC and more about noise-induced hearing loss visit Noise-Induced Hearing Loss | NIDCD

HOW TO CHOOSE HEARING PROTECTION DEVICES

The best way to protect your hearing from noise is to not participate in noisy activities. When avoidance of noise is impossible, use hearing protection devices. Hearing protection devices are worn to reduce the level of sound entering the ear. They do not block out sound completely. Hearing protection that is improperly fit or not worn at the time of exposure will not protect your hearing. Hearing protection devices come with different noise reduction ratings (NRRs). NRRs are usually labeled on the device container and indicate the amount of potential protection the hearing protection device provides. NRRs are measured in decibels (dB). Most hearing protection devices have NRRs that range from 0 dB to 35 dB. NRR is a “best case” rating measured in a laboratory and the actual sound reduction provided by the protector is often much less.  It is best to choose a hearing protector that is comfortable and convenient and that you are willing to wear consistently when exposed to noise.  If you want to know exactly how much noise reduction you are getting, you can have the device “fit-tested” by a hearing professional.

The actual sound reduction is less than the number listed for the device when worn correctly. As a basic guide, subtract 7 from the NRR number, then divide the result by 2 to see how well the device might reduce your exposure. For example, you might wear earplugs with an NRR of 27 dB to a concert where the sound reaches 100 dB. You calculate the actual noise reduction as (27 – 7)/2 = 10. Your earplugs actually reduce the sound by 10 dB, so your exposure (100 dB – 10 dB) is 90 dB. For more information refer to The Occupational Safety and Health Administration Methods for Estimating HPD Attenuation .

The choice of hearing protection device depends on personal preferences of comfort and where it will be worn. How well it works depends on whether it is worn consistently and correctly. The most common types of hearing protection devices include the following:

  • Insert type earplugs: These devices provide an air-tight seal with the ear canal, and are cheap, effective, and easy to use. They can be premolded (pliable devices of fixed proportions), formable (usually made of expandable foam), custom-molded (to fit precisely the size and shape of an individual’s ear canal), and canal caps (earplugs on a flexible plastic or metal band). During quiet times, when not needed, you can leave the canal caps hanging around your neck so they will be easy to find when needed.

To properly insert foam plugs, roll the expandable plugs into a thin, crease-free cylinder. Make sure you get a smooth tube thin enough so that about half the length will fit easily into your ear canal. With your other hand, pull up and back on your ear to straighten out your ear canal, insert the rolled down protector, and then hold it in place a few seconds until the foam expands.

 

                         

 

For more information visit  - “How to Wear Soft Foam Earplugs".

  • Earmuffs come in many models designed to fit most people. They block out noise by completely covering the outer ear. Some earmuffs also include electronic parts to help users communicate or to block sound impulses and / or background noise. However, earmuffs might not work as well for people with heavy beards, sideburns, or those who wear glasses, which can create gaps between the earmuff cushion and ear.

Wearing both earmuffs and earplugs can reduce the sound further. However the NRRs for the two do not add together. For more information refer to The Occupational Safety and Health Administration Methods for Estimating HPD Attenuation.

For a list of available hearing protection devices by type and feature, visit CDC NIOSH Hearing Protector Device Compendium.

For more information on fit testing, visit CDC - NIOSH Science Blog – NIOSH HPD Well-Fit™ : The Future is Fit-Testing. 

You can also get specially made hearing protection devices. They can be styled and sized specifically for a person’s individual ear/ a child, etc., or have special features, such as

  • Custom earplugs molded to fit your ear exactly
  • Earmuffs with built-in radios or communication devices that allow you to listen at a safe level while still protecting you from the loud noise outside (e.g. at sporting events),
  • “Level-dependent” hearing protectors that do not block sound when the environment is quiet, but block loud sounds. These can be very useful for hunters
  •  Lightweight active noise cancellation for reducing low-frequency noise, e.g. in aircraft cabins. These noise cancelling earphones or headphones not only physically block noise in the environment (ambient noise) but also electronically cancel the actual soundwaves. The noise cancelling devices work best with low-pitched droning sounds, such as car and airplane engines, air conditioners, etc.
  • Uniform-attenuation earplugs for musicians and concert attendees. These devices act like turning down the volume on a stereo, i.e., the sound intensity is decreased.

For more information on how to properly wear hearing protection devices, visit http://www.cdc.gov/niosh/topics/noise/choose.html.
