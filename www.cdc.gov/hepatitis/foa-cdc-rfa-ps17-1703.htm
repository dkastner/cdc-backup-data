Funding Opportunity Number (FOA) CDC-RFA-PS17-1703, Strengthening Surveillance in Jurisdictions with High Incidence of Hepatitis C Virus and Hepatitis B Virus Infections

Recommend on Facebook Tweet ShareCompartir

General Information

On November 29, 2016, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention issued a Funding Opportunity Announcement (FOA) to improve active surveillance of hepatitis C virus (HCV) and hepatitis B virus (HBV) in statewide jurisdictions experiencing high rates of incident cases of HCV and/or HBV by supporting implementation of testing, treatment, and prevention services and activities based on surveillance data. This FOA builds upon current HCV and HBV surveillance projects and technical efforts, including CDC-RFA-PS13-1303, Viral Hepatitis – Prevention and Surveillance. This FOA supports the goals of the Action Plan for the Prevention, Care, & Treatment of Viral Hepatitis (2014-2016) [PDF - 100 pages] and the CDC Division of Viral Hepatitis Strategic Plan (2016-2020) [PDF - 17 pages] (DVH Strategic Plan). This is an open competition announcement.

Key Messages

  • CDC recently announced an FOA titled “Strengthening Surveillance in Jurisdictions with High Incidence of Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) and Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) Infections.” The purpose of this funding announcement is to improve active surveillance of hepatitis C virus (HCV) and hepatitis B virus (HBV) in statewide jurisdictions experiencing high rates of incident cases of HCV and/or HBV, and based on these data support implementation of testing, treatment, and prevention services and activities.
  • This FOA supports the goals of the Action Plan for the Prevention, Care, & Treatment of Viral Hepatitis (2014-2016) [PDF - 100 pages] and the CDC Division of Viral Hepatitis Strategic Plan (2016-2020) [PDF - 17 pages] (DVH Strategic Plan).
  • The new FOA builds upon previous activities to enhance and strengthen viral hepatitis surveillance. Awardees will:
    • Work with state departments of health, traditional and non-traditional partner organizations, and CDC’s Division of Viral Hepatitis (DVH) to implement activities to improve viral hepatitis surveillance to produce reliable and estimates of the burden and epidemiology of HCV and HBV infections and to support and evaluate prevention programs. 
    • Work with partners to implement and evaluate interventions to improve HCV and HBV testing, diagnosis, and linkage to care in areas with high prevalence of HCV and/or HBV.
    • Integrate evidence-based HCV and HBV prevention activities into existing public health, clinical care, and community settings.
  • With FOA PS17-1703, CDC will provide funding to support the following core surveillance activities: 
    • Identifying cases of acute and chronic HCV and HBV infections, completing case reports on all identified cases, and notify CDC of all identified cases;
    • Increasing the accuracy of case-based surveillance by supplementing case surveillance data with data from other existing sources to identify and estimate the population at risk for transmission of HCV and HBV;
    • Developing reports and sharing HCV and HBV surveillance data with state and local prevention programs and other partners to support prevention activities; and
    • Detecting and investigating HCV and HBV outbreaks.
  • With FOA PS17-1703, CDC will provide funding to support the following optional surveillance-based activities:
    • Promoting HCV and HBV testing and reporting in settings serving target populations, such as emergency departments and detention/incarceration centers;
    • Evaluating the effectiveness of HCV and HBV testing and linkage to recommended services and treatment at syringe service programs, alcohol and drug treatment venues, and buprenorphine naloxone and other opioid prevention and treatment programs; and
    • Among reported cases, following up targeted HCV and HBV infected persons, especially those with evidence of recent infection, to ensure they are linked to care and treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions

If you have a question about the FOA, please contact Ruth Jiles (dvhsurvellancefoa@cdc.gov). We will post answers to your questions on this webpage. While we may not answer each individual question, answers will be posted for questions that are similar in nature. Please check back regularly for updated FAQs.

  1. What is the purpose of this FOA?
    The purpose of this funding announcement is to improve active surveillance of hepatitis C virus (HCV) and hepatitis B virus (HBV) in statewide jurisdictions experiencing high rates of incident cases of HCV and/or HBV infection and to support the implementation of testing, treatment, and prevention services and activities based on surveillance data.  
  2. Who is eligible for the PS17-1703 FOA?
    Please refer to FOA Eligibility Information for a list of eligible entities and for more information. This FOA provides full and open competition and the following entities are eligible:
    • All State governments or their bona fide agents (includes the District of Columbia, county governments or their bona fide agents, city or township governments, and special district governments, and Native American tribal governments).
    • Independent school districts, public and state controlled institutions of higher education, private institution of higher learning, and private colleges and universities.
    • Nonprofits, for profit organizations other than small businesses, small businesses.
  3. How will awardees be selected?
    Please refer to FOA Review and Selection Process information for a detailed description about how awardees will be selected. Per the FOA:
    • All applications will be reviewed, scored, and ranked by an objective review panel in accordance with the established criteria, as described in the FOA.
    • Awardees will be selected for funding based on score and preference factors.
    • The core and optional surveillance-based components will be reviewed and scored separately.
  4. Are there any selection preferences that will be applied to the process?
    Please refer the FOA for information on Funding Strategy. CDC may fund out of rank order based on the following criteria, using the best data available at the time:
    • Highest HCV and HBV incidence and number of acute infections;
    • To achieve geographic diversity among awardees;
    • Highest HCV or HBV incidence and number of acute infections; and
    • Other supporting evidence indicative of increases in HCV or HBV transmission (i.e. mortality data showing increasing deaths due to drug overdose, data from the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration showing high and/or increasing injection drug use within the jurisdiction, etc). 
  5. Why has CDC changed the focus of the purpose of the funding? 
    CDC made the decision to focus on building core capacity for state-wide surveillance rather than on a specific jurisdiction. Many states are experiencing HCV and HBV epidemics outside (as well as inside) metropolitan areas and therefore will benefit from assistance with strengthening statewide surveillance efforts.
  6. How will Technical Assistance be provided to awardees?
    Please refer to the FOA (see: CDC Program Support to Awardees) for the description of technical assistance that CDC will provide to awardees. CDC will:
    • Provide technical assistance by SMEs in developing and implementing annual work plans for both core and optional activities.
    • Provide technical support, during the first 6 months of the project, in developing a more detailed evaluation and performance measurement plan, including communication of certain key variables to be collected and mechanism of de-identified data transfer to CDC.
    • Provide guidance and technical support for identifying target populations and sampling eligible persons for surveillance of chronic HCV and HBV infections. 
    • Participate in joint monthly conference calls, one awardee meeting per project period, and at least one site visit to each awardee during the project period.
    • Disseminate findings jointly with participating project areas.
    • Monitor and provide feedback on awardee progress in developing and implementing the project, through consultations via routine conference calls, site visits, email, and review of progress reports. 
    • Provide guidance and technical support for project management.
    • Provide technical support for electronic data collection and data transfer to CDC.
    • Provide technical support and consultation for management, analysis, and interpretation of data.
    • Assist with identifying appropriate secondary data sources for conducting viral hepatitis surveillance.
    • Provide at least one annual report that include a summary of the process and outcome performance for all grantees.
  7. Does the plan in the first sentence of the quote below refer to the work plan that is a component of the application or the work plan that will be developed with CDC within the first 6 months of the project?
    “This plan should incorporate local area performance measurement plans, if applicable. This detailed evaluation plan must build on elements stated in the initial work plan, and must be no more than 10 pages for the Core component and 10 pages for the Optional component. Awarded applicants will work with CDC on developing a more detailed evaluation and performance measurement plan within the first 6 months of the project.”
    • (See ii.  Applicant Evaluation and Performance Plan) for modified text.  The 10 page limit, which referenced the work plan that is included in the initial application, was removed.
  8. Will DVH consider applications that only address HBV? In our local health jurisdiction, the HCV and HBV populations are drastically different with HBV being mainly API and the target population of our county’s efforts.
    Because the focus of the FOA is both HCV and HBV, applications that do not include both are likely to be less competitive.
  9. We have a large number of chronic HBV cases, but small number of acute cases. Will addressing only surveillance of chronic and perinatal HBV be able to produce a strong application?
    The entire process of review, scoring, and selecting applications for funding is quite competitive.  Applications that include only chronic and perinatal HBV will actually compete with other applications that include acute and chronic HCV and acute, chronic, and perinatal HBV, and are likely to be less competitive.
  10. Is there a preference for applicants from state health departments relative to local/county jurisdiction public health departments?
    One of the criteria for being responsive to the announcement and for funding is the ability to submit state-wide data.  Basically, state health departments (SHD) are the only entity that submits state-wide surveillance data to CDC.  Therefore, in order for a local health department (LHD) to submit state-wide data, the SHD must give the state-wide data to the LHD and provide a Memorandum of Understanding/Agreement (MOU/MOA), allowing the LHD to submit the state-wide data on behalf of the SHD.
  11. Are non-state applicants required to demonstrate access to *statewide* surveillance data via a Memorandum of Understanding, Memorandum of Agreement, or data-sharing agreement, or only to local data submitted to the state from the city/county jurisdiction that is applying?  
    Non-state applicants must include a MOU or MOA from the state health department that gives the non-state entity access to and permission to submit state-wide data to CDC.
  12. Are large cities, which operate independently of the large state in which it is located, eligible to apply independently of the state?  
    One of the criteria for being responsive to the announcement and for funding is the ability to submit state-wide data.  Basically, state health departments (SHD) are the only entity that submits state-wide surveillance data to CDC.  Large city and/or county health departments are encouraged to work with their state health department to submit one comprehensive application or to obtain permission to submit state-wide data on behalf of the SHD.
  13. Is there any chance of eliminating the requirement for a logic model?  There is really nothing unique to each state that is different than the program logic model that was included in the RFA.  Elimination of the logic model will provide more room for pertinent information?  
    The inclusion of a logic model in the RFA and requirement for the logic model in the applications are essential for all CDC FOAs and applications.
  14. Would CDC consider responsive an application that proposed focusing on hepatitis C in the optional activities rather than both hepatitis B and hepatitis C based on state morbidity and to make effective use of limited public health resources?  
    Yes
  15. Are the page limits for the core and optional work plans (5 and 4 pages, respectively) inclusive of the detailed and high-level work plans?   
    The page limitations for the Project Narrative, including Background, Approach, Applicant Evaluation and Performance Measurement Plan, Organizational Capacity of Applicant to Implement the Approach, and Work Plan, is 20 pages.  Page limits for Applicant Evaluation and Performance Measurement Plan, Organizational Capacity of Applicant to Implement the Approach, and Work Plan have been removed.
  16. Does CDC expect that funded jurisdictions will obtain complete (as defined in the FOA) demographic, geographic, and, specifically risk information and signs/symptoms information for both acute and chronic cases?  
    We recognize the difficulty in obtaining complete demographic, geographic, and risk information, especially for chronic cases.  While the goals established in the FOA are specifically for acute disease, it is also desirable to obtain complete demographic and geographic information for chronic case reports.
  17. What is CDC’s definition of "statewide jurisdictions experiencing high rates of incident cases of HCV, HBV, or both"?  
    CDC defines statewide rates that are higher than the national rate as “high” rates of incident cases.
  18. How might the cutoff rates for HCV and/or HBV be made?
    Assuming the application is “responsive”, scoring will be made based on both the quality of application and high rates of HCV and/or HBV.
  19. If hepatitis B/C test result reporting is already mandated throughout the state, is a letter of support required from all local health jurisdictions that will be reporting their data to the state public health system (see pg. 12 of FOA)? Would a copy of regulations requiring provider and laboratory reporting to locals and reporting from locals to the state be sufficient to demonstrate that all positive test results are already submitted via ELR/public health reporting?
    • Letters of support are not required from local health jurisdictions that report data to the state public health system, as mandated by the state.
    • A copy of state regulations, requiring provider and laboratory reporting to local health jurisdictions and reporting from locals to the state health jurisdiction, may be included in the Appendix.
  20. Are applicants required/encouraged to include travel to a grantees’ meeting in the annual budget? (If state staff are to attend a grantee meeting, it needs to be required in writing by CDC.)  
    Funds for travel to a grantee meeting are not included in the current FOA.
  21. Can higher incident areas and/or counties within a state be considered and targeted in the FOA, or is the jurisdiction of interest across the entire state?  
    The focus of the FOA is states with high rates of HCV, HBV, or both.  Targeting specific areas within the high rate state may be a way to focus optional surveillance-based activities.
  22. What is the format for reporting cases through The CDC Secure Access Management Services (SAMS)?
    The CDC SAMS portal is a web site designed to provide centralized access to public health information and computer applications operated by the CDC. Data collected under this surveillance activity that cannot be transmitted to CDC via NEDSS/NETSS will instead be transmitted via CDC-SAMS.  CDC-DVH will provide the surveillance data management or designated point of contact in each of the funded sites with instructions on how to register with CDC-SAMS, and how to use the DVH data layout to standardize data elements and data formats. Detailed instructions will be provided on how to upload the monthly data files directly to CDC-DVH.
  23. Is the purchase of testing supplies/kits allowed?  
    The purchase of testing kits is not authorized under Core activities.  However, contractual agreement to actualize optional components may include purchase of testing kits.
