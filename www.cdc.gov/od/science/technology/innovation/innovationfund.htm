CDC Innovation Fund (iFund)

Recommend on Facebook Tweet ShareCompartir

The CDC Innovation Fund (iFund) seeks to promote the inventiveness and creativity of the CDC community in the design and development of new innovations which show promise for making a substantial impact on public health and how we accomplish our mission. The iFund provides intramural funding and support to CDC staff to develop initial proof of concept ‘prototypes’ and pilot projects, or scale up more mature projects that have been proven effective through pilot or replication studies.

CDC iFund Provides

  • Intramural funding with past awards around $100,000
  • Up to a 15 month project period (as defined by the proposal)
  • Multi-day informational training on entrepreneurial methodologies and design principles
  • Guidance and technical support specific to project implementation needs from CDC Innovation Lab (I-Lab) staff

Since its inception in 2011, the iFund has supported and financed over 60 mission-driven projects. For FY2016, the Office of Innovation and Technology received over 82 proposals for consideration. The teams selected for FY2016 iFund support include one project co-funded with the HHS Ventures Fund.

The FY2016 selected individuals, projects, and CIOs are listed below.

Development of a portable multi-sensor device to conduct remote, periodic air sampling for investigating city-wide air pollution complaints

Collaborators: Greg Zarus, Diane Jackson, Lynn Wilder, and Custodio Muianga

Communities and local officials often request investigation of hazardous air pollution that they believe is harming their health. Air pollution events are usually episodic. It’s impractical and expensive to use continuous monitors or 24 hour samplers. Using new sensor technology, this team will build and test inexpensive battery-powered sampling devices that can detect multiple chemicals under a variety of atmospheric conditions, turn on a sampler from the National Institute of Occupational Safety and Health, collect a laboratory sample, and report that it's time to have that sample analyzed.

Field validation of digital microfluidics (DMF)-powered immunoassays for the detection of measles and rubella infection and immunity

Collaborators: Eugene Lam, Heather Scobie, Paul Rota, and Aaron Wheeler 

Digital microfluidics (DMF) is a liquid handling method that can manipulate droplets on exceptionally small and inexpensive devices. We have established that it can perform laboratory-quality immunoassays. In partnership with the World Health Organization and the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) Ministry of Health, the team will evaluate the DMF-powered assays for measles and rubella (MR) using 600 specimens collected in DRC. If successful, this technology can enable early detection and response of disease outbreaks, contributing to the MR elimination goals worldwide.

Better Integration through Innovation (BITI): Use of an innovative multiplex antibody screening assay to improve maternal and child health (MCH)

Collaborators: Bharat Parekh, Yetunde Fakile, Ernest Yufenyuy, Diana Martin, Tun Ye, and Mary Kamb

Laboratory assays capable of simultaneously detecting multiple viruses or bacteria using a single specimen are less invasive, easier to collect and cost effective. Such assays can also support more cost-effective disease surveillance in resource limited settings. The team will develop a new assay specifically aimed at diagnosing pathogens such as HIV, syphilis, hepatitis C, herpes simplex 2, and susceptibility to rubella, hepatitis B, or other organisms during pregnancy affecting delivery of a healthy newborn.

Use of malaria parasite-specific metabolites as biomarkers for development of a sensitive, low-cost, rapid, simple, and field deployable non-invasive diagnostic for malaria infection

Collaborators: Ya Ping Shi, Sheila Sergent, Xichun Zhou, Scott Angus, and Gus Dinovo

Current morphologic, antigen, and DNA-based malaria diagnosis platforms require blood sampling and have limitations for large-scale and real-time screening of asymptomatic and low density malaria infection at the community level; this presents a challenge for global malaria elimination programs. The team will use malaria parasite-specific metabolites as biomarkers for development of a sensitive, low-cost, rapid, and simple non-invasive diagnostic tool to address this challenge. A novel diagnostic tool with this profile will have an enormous impact on global malaria elimination.

Inexpensive Counterfeit Drug Identification Device (CoDI)

Collaborators: Mike Green, Isabel Swamidoss, Babita Ganguly, and Maria Paul

Poor quality drugs are a significant threat to global health as well as the health of the individual. On the global scale, poor quality antimicrobials containing sub-therapeutic amounts of active ingredients contribute to microbial resistance, while the individual may suffer by taking medicines containing wrong or even no active ingredients. The team has designed a simple and cheap device to detect falsified/degraded/expired drug. A prototype device has been fielded in Ghana with positive results. The team will continue to improve the prototype device and seek a partner to accelerate commercialization.

More rapid autism classification for public health: Leveraging algorithms to review medical records and reduce labor of estimating the prevalence of autism in the US

Collaborators: Matthew Maenner, Chad Heilig, Scott Lee, Nicole Dowling, and Maureen Durkin

There is currently no definitive biomarker for autism and professionals use a variety of standards and tools for diagnosing the condition. A major challenge for autism prevalence studies is establishing a reliable definition of autism that can be applied consistently to each child. The team will work toward several major goals: 1) develop more refined classification algorithms using recently developed machine-learning and natural language processing tools, such as paragraph vectors and convolutional neural networks; 2) evaluate the accuracy of the classification algorithms through testing at multiple sites and across time to establish how well an algorithm can perform using evaluations from different parts of the country or those written in different years; and 3) make the tools and processes scalable across the agency.

Identification of Metabolic Conjugates to Detect Opioid Exposure

Collaborators: Melissa Carter, Thomas Mathews, Samantha Isenberg, Mike Mojica, Rebecca Shaner, and Rudolph Johnson

The team will develop a data-dependent high resolution tandem mass spectrometry method to screen for metabolic conjugates to opioids from human urine. Opioids, such as heroin, claim thousands of lives every year; in 2013, over 8,000 individuals overdosed on heroin alone. Despite their structural diversity, liver enzymes conjugate most opioids to a small number of water soluble biomolecules for excretion in urine. Screening for fragmentation events consistent with the loss of these biomolecules will rapidly identify exposure regardless of the parent opioid.

Death-on-FHIR: Using New Technologies to Help Improve the Accuracy of Mortality Data and Support Real-Time Surveillance

Collaborators: Paula Braun, Mark Braunstein (Georgia Tech), Glenn Copeland (Michigan), Michelle Williamson, and Hetty Khan

Mortality data are used to identify public health threats, determine what interventions work best for whom, guide health-related policy decisions, and track cost-effectiveness outcomes in real-world settings. In this project, our team is further developing a tool to help physicians better understand what sequence of diseases led to death. Our goals are to improve the timeliness, completeness, and accuracy of data captured on death certificates, and to use this surveillance data to help respond to evolving health threats in real-time.

NIOSH- Mini Baghouse Technology to Control Silica Dust at Fracking Sites

Collaborators: Eric Esswein and Arthur Miller

CDC researchers have invented and completed three successful Phase I field trials of an engineering control that retrofits tens of thousands of machines used in oil and gas fracking that emit highly toxic silica dusts into the air. Funding is required for Phase II field trials of the patent-pending technology that effectively (99%) controls silica dust generated during fracking. Exposure to respirable silica, an occupational carcinogen and the cause of silicosis (an incurable lung disease), is the most serious occupational health hazard in U.S. oil and gas extraction today.
