Global Health Security Agenda: Frequently Asked Questions

Recommend on Facebook Tweet ShareCompartir

Global health security aims to protect the well-being of people around the world by preventing, detecting, and responding to public health threats. Below are answers to frequently asked questions about global health security as it relates to the Global Health Security Agenda.

  • Global Health Security (GHS) Basics
  • GHS Agenda Basics
  • U.S. Supported Countries
  • GHS Agenda and Ebola
  • Funding
  • Targets and Tracking Progress
  • Connections with Other Global Programs

Global Health Security (GHS) Basics

  1. What is global health security?

    The World Health Organization (WHO) defines global health security as the “activities required…to reduce the vulnerability of people around the world to new, acute, or rapidly spreading risk to health, particularly those that threaten to cross international borders.”[1]

    Global health security describes the capacities required for countries to prepare for and respond to public health threats and reduce the risk of these threats crossing borders. The global threat of diseases such as Ebola and Middle East Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus (MERS-CoV) spreading from person to person and across borders poses a public health risk to all countries. The risk is especially high for low-and middle-resource countries with less-developed systems to prevent, detect, and rapidly respond to emerging and re-emerging public health threats.

  2. What is CDC’s role with global health security?

    CDC has extensive experience in public health emergency management, disease tracking, surveillance systems, training, infection control, and laboratory efforts targeting high hazard pathogens, including Ebola. CDC's overseas platforms and the agency’s direct relationships with Ministries of Health facilitate rapid responses to infectious diseases and other public health threats.

    Additionally, to address the urgent need for stronger health systems worldwide, CDC’s Division of Global Health Protection is leading the agency’s efforts to implement the Global Health Security Agenda along with other U.S. agencies and global partners.

GHS Agenda Basics

  1. What does the GHS Agenda do?

    The Global Health Security Agenda (GHS Agenda) is a unifying framework to improve our global response to disease outbreaks and close gaps in surveillance and response so that disease threats are stopped at the earliest possible opportunity. It builds upon existing programs and policies to improve health and spurs progress toward full implementation of the World Health Organization’s (WHO) International Health Regulations 2005 (IHR), the World Organization for Animal Health (OIE) Performance of Veterinary Services (PVS) Pathway, and other relevant global health security frameworks.[2]

    Specifically, the GHS Agenda serves to stimulate investment and empowers countries, international organizations, and civil society to work with one another to achieve focused goals and measures:

    • PREVENT avoidable epidemics, including naturally occurring outbreaks and intentional or accidental releases.
    • DETECT threats early, including detecting, characterizing, and transparently reporting emerging biological threats at the earliest possible moment.
    • RESPOND rapidly and effectively to biological threats of international concern.
  2. What activities are covered by the GHS Agenda?

    Eleven activities, called “Action Packages”, were identified following the May 2014 GHS Agenda Commitment Development meeting in Helsinki, Finland. These activities facilitate regional and global collaboration toward specific GHS Agenda objectives and targets that fall under Prevent, Detect, and Respond goals. They are:

    • Prevent:
      • Antimicrobial Resistance
      • Zoonotic Diseases
      • Biosafety Biosecurity
      • Immunization
    • Detect:
      • National Laboratory System
      • Real-time Surveillance
      • Reporting
      • Workforce Development
    • Respond:
      • Emergency Operations Centers
      • Linking Public Health with Law and Rapid Response
      • Medical Countermeasures and Personal Deployment

  3. What is CDC’s role with the GHS Agenda?

    CDC is working directly with countries through the GHS Agenda to improve their ability to prevent, detect, and respond to health threats. The GHS Agenda expands on the IHR guidance by providing specific and measurable targets in 11 areas. While CDC will support the implementation of all of these targets, we are focusing on a minimum of four key public health components of the GHS Agenda as the cornerstone of our strategy:

    • National Laboratory Systems – To improve diagnostic capacity and provide data to assess disease trends, guide use of resources, and evaluate whether interventions are working. 
    • Real-Time Surveillance – To provide early and accurate identification of potential public health emergencies.
    • Workforce Development – To ensure well-trained public health workers to meet relevant International Health Regulations (IHR) core competencies.
    • Emergency Operations Centers – To establish public health Emergency Operations Centers (EOC) functioning according to minimum common standards; maintaining trained rapid response teams and “real-time” biosurveillance laboratory networks and information systems; and training EOC staff capable of activating a coordinated emergency response within 120 minutes of the identification of a public health emergency.

    CDC will continue to apply its ‘boots on the ground’ approach that has served the agency well in every prior global public health response.

  4. What organizations will be involved in the GHS Agenda?

    In addition to the 48 countries that have committed to the GHS Agenda, the World Health Organization (WHO), the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) of the United Nations and the World Organization for Animal Health (OIE), are involved in the Agenda. Within the U.S. Government, the Departments of Agriculture, Defense, Health and Human Services, State and United States Agency for International Development (USAID) are playing significant roles.

  5. What is the leadership structure for the GHS Agenda?

    The GHS Agenda Steering Group is charged with tracking progress, identifying challenges, and overseeing implementation for achieving the objectives of the GHS Agenda. The GHS Agenda Steering Group will be comprised of ten countries with a rotating Chairmanship. The Steering Group will include: Canada, Chile, Finland, India, Indonesia, Italy, Kenya, the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, the Republic of Korea, and the United States.

    For 2015, Finland will chair the Steering Group. The United States served as Chair during 2014, and Indonesia will serve as Chair in 2016. Additionally, WHO, FAO, and OIE will serve as permanent advisors to the GHS Agenda Steering Group and will each designate a representative to serve in that role.

  6. How are countries committing to GHS Agenda?

    In addition to participating in the Steering Committee, countries have also been encouraged to make commitments to the Action Packages. Countries can either lead an Action Package or contribute based on their level of experience and expertise in the specific activities. This commits the country to continue to improve its own capacity in this area, as well as work with other countries to develop action plans and share their expertise with other countries that have gaps.

  7. What countries have committed?

    For its part, the United States has committed to assisting at least 30 countries over five years to achieve the objectives of the GHS Agenda and has placed a priority for our actions on improving laboratory systems, combating antibiotic resistant bacteria, supporting biosafety and biosecurity on a global basis, enhancing public health workforce development, and building surveillance systems.

    Since February 2014, 48 countries have made over 100 new commitments to implement the 11 Action Packages. Every country’s contribution should reflect its own priorities and needs within the broader GHS Agenda. Each country will be encouraged to develop measurable commitments to prevention, detection, and response.

  8. How will partners be involved?

    No one country can ensure global health security alone. There is a commitment to partnerships with other nations, international organizations, and civil society and across a variety of sectors to make concrete progress toward a world safe and secure from the threat of infectious diseases. Countries were invited to partner in a collective, international effort that will take place over the next five years to accelerate progress to prevent, detect, and respond to infectious disease threats.

    • Every country’s contribution should reflect its own priorities and needs within the broader GHS Agenda. Each country will be encouraged to develop measurable commitments to prevention, detection, and response.
    • Some countries will undertake domestic activities to improve their nation’s health security capacity.
    • Others will assist neighboring countries or strengthen global health security institutions such as WHO, FAO, and OIE to build or strengthen capacities.
    • Some may share lessons learned and foster transparency and information sharing during public health emergencies.

U.S. Supported Countries

  1. In which countries will CDC support global health security implementation activities?

    CDC is working with over 30 U.S. partner countries to improve their ability to prevent, detect and respond to infectious disease threats. The first stage in this process is to develop country plans that will help reach GHS Agenda action packages and targets. In certain countries, we are establishing CDC offices to achieve these goals in coordination with local governments and other partners.

    As of January 2016, U.S. partner countries are: Bangladesh, Burkina Faso, Cambodia, Cameroon, Cote d’Ivoire, Democratic Republic of Congo, Ethiopia, Georgia, Ghana, Guinea, Haiti, India, Indonesia, Jordan, Kazakhstan, Kenya, Laos, Liberia, Malaysia, Mali, Mozambique, Pakistan, Peru, Rwanda, Senegal, Sierra Leone, Tanzania, Thailand, Uganda, Ukraine, and Vietnam. In addition, we plan to partner with the Caribbean Community (CARICOM) to strengthen regional capacity.

GHS Agenda and Ebola

  1. How has Ebola changed the Global Health Security Agenda?

    Ebola is precisely the kind of health threat the GHS Agenda could have prevented. The same activities that are necessary to combat the spread of Ebola will reduce the potential for future outbreaks of infectious diseases that could follow a similarly devastating, costly, and destabilizing trajectory.

    • The Ebola response demonstrates the need to establish global health security capacity in vulnerable countries before illnesses and outbreaks become epidemics. The Ebola outbreak has proven the value of standing up emergency operations centers; providing equipment and training needed to test patients and report data in real-time; providing safe and secure laboratory capacity; and developing a trained workforce to track and end outbreaks before they become epidemics.
    • In Liberia, for example, CDC supported the Ministry of Health and Social Welfare to establish an Incident Management System to align national response structure with well-recognized emergency management principles[3], creating rapid response teams to quickly get to hard to reach communities to safely isolate the sick, and streamlining laboratory systems to quickly test samples so patients could be treated in Ebola Treatment Units. As Liberia’s Ebola cases have dramatically decreased, the systems now in place in Liberia can help prevent, detect and respond to the next disease outbreak.

  2. What are the plans on getting Ebola to zero cases in West Africa?

    "The largest, biggest risk is that it continues to fester and continues to spread at a low rate, which means it could flare up at any time. We have to get to zero and then stay at zero and that's going to require monitoring, surveillance."

    Dr. Thomas Frieden
    CDC Director
    January 13, 2015

    CDC’s Ebola response priority objectives are: To interrupt Ebola transmission in affected countries and prevent Ebola transmission in other countries. For the affected countries, we are quickly identifying cases, tracking people who came in contact with infected individuals, and getting patients into treatment. We are also providing additional healthcare resources to the affected countries.

    As we track the epidemic’s declining cases (available here), the focus shifts to diligently finding these cases and tracing all contacts to ensure that there are no additional ‘flare ups’ of disease that could reignite the outbreak. As the number of cases continues to drop, we will provide surveillance support to the affected countries, and countries within the region, to ensure that all cases are identified. This will also require close work with partners and communities to ensure that all cases are reported.

    Discussions with country leaders and partners have begun for the ‘recovery phase’ planning in the affected countries, Guinea, Liberia and Sierra Leone, and other countries in West Africa. This includes support to the health systems, and implementation of the GHS to ensure that they have the capacity to build on lessons learned and resources provided during the outbreak to prevent, detect and respond to outbreaks into the future. See CDC Grand Rounds: Shifts in Global Health Security: Lessons from Ebola, aired on September 29, 2015, to learn more about how our global experience in the Ebola epidemic is informing actions taken to further global health security.

Funding

  1. What resources are available to support global health security, including new funding to CDC?

    In FY 2015 the U.S. CDC received an appropriation to build core public health capacities around the world to ensure that future outbreaks are prevented where possible, detected quickly and accurately, and responded to rapidly. Other USG Agencies are also providing support to global health security activities using appropriated funds.

    In addition to the United States, other countries have also committed to supporting the GHS Agenda through funding, technical support, or other types of assistance.

  2. How is the Ebola Emergency Funding Request, which was passed along with the FY 2015 Omnibus, going to support the GHS Agenda?

    Within this appropriation, CDC received $597 million to advance the GHS Agenda, USAID received over $200 million, and State Department received additional funds to be expended over a period of between 3-5 years. This funding was provided to prevent the spread of Ebola and to prevent future outbreaks of Ebola and other infectious diseases before they become epidemics in high-priority countries around the world.

Targets and Tracking Progress

  1. What are the U.S. Government Targets for the GHS Agenda?

    Along with the Action Packages, the U.S. Government has established targets to achieve the goals of the GHS Agenda. The “Real Time Surveillance” Action Package includes 2 U.S. Government Targets. The targets include:

    Prevent

    Detect

    Respond

    Antimicrobial Resistance

    National Laboratory Systems

    Emergency Operations Centers

    Zoonotic Diseases

    Syndromic Surveillance

    Linking Public Health and Law Enforcement

    Biosafety and Biosecurity

    Linked Data Systems

    Medical Countermeasures

    Immunization

    Global Health Security Reporting

     

     

    Workforce Development

     

  2. How are we going to measure progress or success?

    The overall goal is to accelerate our progress toward a world safe and secure from infectious disease threats, in direct support of the International Health Regulations 2005 (IHR). Substantial progress has already been made toward meeting the goals of the IHR, which were agreed to by all the Member States of the WHO. GHS Agenda Steering Committee countries are working towards implementing an assessment process that will:

    • Develop a baseline measure of a country’s capacity in the action package areas,
    • Serve as a planning tool for identifying needs within a country, and
    • Over time, track progress based on new activities.

  3. What is an example of global health security success?

    Nigeria is an example of success in long term global health security investments. Infrastructure put in place several years ago led to a rapid Ebola response coordinated by Nigeria’s Emergency Operations Center. Nigeria established its Incident Management System and Emergency Operations Center in October 2012 as part of its emergency plan for the global polio eradication initiative. This capacity-building work to fight polio was supported by CDC and the private sector, including Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, Dangote Foundation, Clinton Health Access Initiative, and McKinsey. Nigeria used this infrastructure developed under the polio eradication response to stand up its EOC and Incident Management Structure rapidly after first importation of an Ebola case from Liberia. CDC-trained disease detectives from Nigeria’s Field Epidemiology Training Program (FETP) were also put into action to respond.

    Use of the physical infrastructure and skills learned to fight polio allowed for a rapid, coordinated response to identify potential cases, isolate them, and trace contacts. Ultimately, the facilities developed, skills acquired, and lessons learned from global investments to eradicate polio averted the enormous potential for an uncontrolled outbreak in Africa’s most populous city of Lagos.[4]

Connections with Other Global Programs

  1. Is this effort duplicative across USG agencies?

    No. this effort is not duplicative across USG agencies. Each part of the United States’ efforts brings unique technical expertise and relationships to the table with a specific mandate and focus area. The GHS Agenda will capitalize on these unique agency strengths.

  2. How does this effort accelerate progress towards the IHR? What makes this effort different?

    The GHS Agenda is actualizing the goals of global IHR implementation by engaging the highest levels of government and sectors not previously focused on IHR, such as security, foreign affairs, defense, and agriculture. It establishes new, clear targets and a process for monitoring and driving progress.

  3. How does this complement other existing efforts, such as GHSI, G8GP or other existing efforts?

    This effort builds upon but does not replace other partnerships or frameworks on global health security – the GHS Agenda is intended to strengthen and extend beyond them.

    • For example, the Global Health Security Initiative (GHSI) is a partnership among a small number of health ministries. While GHSI is important, the GHS Agenda is especially focused on engaging more countries with an emphasis on multi-sectoral engagement.
    • The Global Partnership Against the Spread of Weapons and Materials of Mass Destruction (G8GP) is another important collaboration, which is focused on a different set of global health threats and involves fewer countries than the GHS Agenda is engaging.
