What We Do: Prepare Countries for Global Health Threats

Global health security is a term used to describe the efforts countries are making to prevent, detect, and respond to infectious diseases and other public health threats and to reduce the risk of these threats crossing borders. Our division helps protect the health of Americans and global populations by working with partner countries to build the capacities needed to identify and address outbreaks before they become epidemics.

In our interconnected world, we are only as safe as the most fragile state. This is a lesson that became clear to the world during the outbreak of severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) in 2003. After SARS, in 2005, all the member states of the World Health Organization (WHO) came together to revise and commit to a set of health policy rules called the International Health Regulations (IHR).1. The IHR are a legally binding framework countries are using to prevent and control global health threats while protecting international travel and trade.

An example of the urgent need for everyone to comply with the IHR and strengthen public health systems is the Ebola epidemic in West Africa. It has shown us the importance of setting out clear, attainable, and measurable goals for global health security. Today, building on the IHR, we are leading efforts to implement the CDC and the Global Health Security Agenda (GHS Agenda), a global initiative to accelerate progress toward strengthening public health systems around the world to ensure that countries can rapidly detect and contain infectious disease threats as closely as possible to their source.

How We Do It

Global Health Security Fact Sheet [543 KB, 2 pages]

The Division of Global Health Protection (DGHP) supports CDC global efforts to strengthen public health systems abroad and build essential infrastructure in host countries.

We lead CDC’s global health security engagement with other U.S. government agencies and programs; multilateral institutions such as the World Health Organization (WHO) and the World Bank; ministries of health; and other international organizations to promote mutual strategies, research, and policies. We work closely with CDC key programs and organize expertise within CDC and beyond to achieve our global commitment to the CDC and the Global Health Security Agenda. We operate at the intersection of public health, defense, development, and diplomacy, working with domestic and international programs across the U.S. government to support partner countries by:

  • Building capacity for disease surveillance and outbreak response
  • Strengthening preparedness and emergency management capabilities
  • Developing information technology tools for disease surveillance and reporting
  • Supporting the intersection of public health and national security interests
  • Developing and monitoring disease surveillance systems
  • Establishing routine surveillance for priority diseases
  • Promoting the development of safe laboratory systems and diagnostics

Why It’s Important

Despite advances in technology and knowledge, the threat of diseases spreading from person to person and across borders poses a public health risk to all countries. The risk is especially high for low- and middle-resource countries with less-developed systems to prevent, detect, and rapidly respond to emerging and re-emerging public health threats.

As a result, dangerous gaps in capacities for data collection and analysis, surveillance and response still exist. This "perfect storm" of global health vulnerability is defined by:

  • New diseases that emerge and spread, such as MERS-CoV and influenza H7N9
  • Increased travel and trade between countries
  • Rise of diseases that are drug resistant
  • Dangerous pathogens that may be released, either accidentally or deliberately

No country can protect itself, by itself. We need to look out for one another, for the sake of us all.

Where We Work

CDC is currently helping partner countries meet their global health security targets, supporting the U.S. goal of assisting 31 countries over the next 5 years. As of January 2016, the 31 partner countries of the United States are: Bangladesh, Burkina Faso, Cambodia, Cameroon, Cote d’Ivoire, Democratic Republic of Congo, Ethiopia, Georgia, Ghana, Guinea, Haiti, India, Indonesia, Jordan, Kazakhstan, Kenya, Laos, Liberia, Malaysia, Mali, Mozambique, Pakistan, Peru, Rwanda, Senegal, Sierra Leone, Tanzania, Thailand, Uganda, Ukraine, and Vietnam.  In addition, we plan to partner with the Caribbean Community (CARICOM) to strengthen regional capacity.

We are also helping to establish disease detection and response capabilities in 8 countries identified as at-risk from the Ebola epidemic (high-risk, non-affected countries, or HRNA). These countries are: Benin, Democratic Republic of the Congo, Gambia, Ghana, Guinea-Bissau, Mauritania, Nigeria, and Togo.

Finally, we continue to strengthen Global Health Security in 6 countries who began activities in 2014 in partnership with the Department of Defense’s Defense Threat Reduction Agency. These countries are: Georgia, Jordan, Kazakhstan, Philippines, South Africa and Thailand.

Fast Facts

  • The concept of global health security is not new: As early as the 14th Century, people used quarantine to keep diseases like the plague from spreading across borders
  • Since 2005, WHO has formally declared a total of 4 Public Health Events of International Concern: the H1N1 influenza pandemic in 2009; the re-emergence of wild poliovirus in 2014; the West Africa Ebola epidemic in 2014; and Zika virus in 2016.
  • The Global Health Security Agenda provides 11 measurable targets in different areas to help build and sustain public health capacity
  • 48 countries, including the U.S., have committed to the GHS Agenda

Our Organization

We integrate programs and experts in partnership with host countries to build capacities in surveillance, disease detection and outbreak response, including:

  • Global Disease Detection (GDD) Operations Center
  • Integrated Disease Surveillance and Response (IDSR)
  • Global Public Health Informatics Program (GPHIP)
  • Country Programs & Partnerships
