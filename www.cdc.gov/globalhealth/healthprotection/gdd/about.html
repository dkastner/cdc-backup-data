What We Do: Advance the Science of Global Public Health

The Global Disease Detection program (GDD) promotes scientific discovery by partnering with host countries to identify new health threats and reduce the burden of infectious diseases. GDD is CDC’s principal program for advancing the science behind global health security. With over a decade of experience between SARS and Ebola -- and over 1700 outbreaks and public health emergencies responded to – the GDD program is critical to helping countries meet their commitments to the International Health Regulations and the Global Health Security Agenda.

As of today, GDD has supported over 50 countries through its 10 Centers worldwide. Centers work with countries to build and strengthen public health capacity. We do this through:

  • Detecting and responding to emerging infectious diseases
  • Developing a global workforce
  • Enhancing laboratory systems
  • Increasing preparedness and communication activities
  • Addressing the animal and human interface responsible for zoonotic infections
  • Conducting public health research

How We Do It

There are two main ways the GDD program uncovers disease threats and helps countries respond to an outbreak:

  • From the GDD Centers located in 10 countries around the world
  • From the GDD Operations Center located at CDC headquarters in Atlanta

The GDD Operations Center monitors outbreaks across the globe and assesses their potential risk to the global community.

The 10 GDD Centers serve as a platform for providing technical support and scientific collaboration at the request of partner countries. GDD Centers:

  • Train scientists to think independently
  • Support research opportunities
  • Contribute to the scientific knowledge base through surveillance and laboratory activities

Centers also participate in a CDC surveillance and research network that extends to multiple sites across the globe. Programs across CDC use this network to track diseases and syndromes, including acute febrile illness (AFI), antimicrobial resistance (AMR), and respiratory diseases.   

The GDD program continuously improves as we identify areas of need and new opportunities to provide technical assistance. Beginning in 2006, the GDD program implemented a comprehensive monitoring and evaluation framework to measure progress in five key areas: outbreak response, pathogen discovery, training, surveillance, and networking.

Why It’s Important

Threats posed by emerging pandemics and other infectious diseases remain a challenge to global health security, endangering economies and decreasing political stability. We work with countries to strengthen core capacities, so that we can find and stop emerging infectious diseases close to the source. GDD Centers work alongside ministries of health to not only keep people in their regions safe, but also to protect against the spread of disease to the U.S. and other countries.

Additionally, results of studies supported by GDD Centers can lead to policy change within countries. For example, scientists at the GDD Center in Kazakhstan worked closely with country partners to help them understand the science behind vaccination programs and the epidemiology of their population. Based on this information, the government of Kazakhstan issued a new policy to promote influenza vaccination for pregnant women and added an accompanying line item in the national budget to support this intervention and save lives.

Where We Work

A central focus of GDD is the establishment and expansion of GDD Centers, particularly in low-resource locations. CDC operates GDD Centers in 1) Bangladesh, 2) Central America (Guatemala), 3) Central Asia (Kazakhstan), 4) China, 5) Egypt, 6) India, 7) Kenya, 8) South Africa, 9) the South Caucasus (country of Georgia), and 10) Thailand. Six of these centers are located in (or are responsible for regional coverage of) countries named by the United States as partner countries for the Global Health Security Agenda. Fast Facts

  • From 2006-2015, GDD Centers responded to over 1,900 disease outbreaks and other public health emergencies, with nearly 2/3 of responses initiated within 24 hours of receiving a request for assistance.
  • GDD Centers have discovered 12 pathogens that were new to the world and detected 61 pathogens that were new to the region in which they were discovered.
  • GDD laboratory staff have helped establish 294 new diagnostic tests in 59 countries, improving disease detection capability and contributing to faster response times.
  • Thanks to the GDD Centers, over 75 million people are covered by surveillance for important diseases and syndromes.
  • More than 700 epidemiologists and laboratorians have graduated from the Field Epidemiology Training Program (FETP) in partnership with GDD Centers.
