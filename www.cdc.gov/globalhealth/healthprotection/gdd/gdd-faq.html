Global Disease Detection Program: Frequently Asked Questions

Recommend on Facebook Tweet ShareCompartir

To learn more about the Global Disease Detection program, browse the topics below.

GDD Program Basics

How is the GDD program helping countries?

GDD helps countries build capacity to detect and respond to potential health threats. While CDC remains vigilant and ready to support responses to public health emergencies when they occur, we must also help countries build the systems they need to find and stop outbreaks.

Another key factor in the program’s work is establishing strong relationships in countries. Building relationships takes time, and our GDD Centers are part of a long-standing partnership with host countries, organized around each country’s priorities and CDC’s technical experience.

How many countries does the GDD program currently support?

Since its beginnings in 2004, the program has supported over 50 countries through its 10 Centers. Assistance includes outbreak response, laboratory and pathogen discovery, training, surveillance, and networking. For example, laboratory staff have helped establish 289 new diagnostic tests in 59 countries, which improved disease detection capability and contributed to faster response times.

Learn more about our centers and where we work.

What types of diseases does the GDD program currently watch for?

The GDD program conducts surveillance and provides laboratory detection for all types of diseases – from common diseases to those we don’t even know exist yet. Our GDD Centers have discovered 12 pathogens that were new to the world and detected 60 pathogens that were new to the region in which they were discovered. The main syndromes we watch for fall into the following categories:

  • Hospital-acquired infections
  • Blood stream infections
  • Diarrheal disease
  • Acute febrile illness 
  • Acute neurologic disease 
  • Acute Jaundice Syndrome 
  • Influenza-like illnesses 
  • Severe acute respiratory infections
  • Tuberculosis 
  • Zoonotic diseases

What are the GDD program’s biggest successes? Biggest challenges?

In some ways, the biggest successes and biggest challenges of the GDD program both stem from the same thing: our ability to help countries be better prepared to prevent, detect, and respond to public health threats.

Scarcely a week goes by without a grim reminder in the media of infectious disease emergence or re-emergence in the United States and around the world. As demonstrated with the SARS outbreak in 2003 and the Ebola epidemic in 2014, a disease threat in one country is a threat to all countries, and the world must have effective prevention and control measures. As we look at the history of the GDD Centers, we see examples of new diseases detected before they became significant threats, as well as faster response to many endemic diseases because of the capacity we helped to build in the country. Because of the importance of being able to detect health threats as close to the source as possible, successes come when we have laboratory capacity in place. Countries can test rapidly and feed information into surveillance systems that then inform public health leadership on what they need to do to protect their citizens.

The biggest challenge is the unknown. As we are working with countries to prepare for the next public health threat, we don’t know what the disease will be, when it will strike, or where it will happen. That’s what makes our work so important. We need the lab systems in place to figure out what pathogens could be emerging as new diseases. We need the surveillance networks in place to know what diseases are naturally occurring and at what levels, so that we know when there is a “blip” that could signal a new threat. And we need the reach of the network of GDD Centers to respond more quickly to diseases within their region.

How is the GDD program funded?

To answer this question, it’s important to understand the federal budget process. Annually, CDC works with the Department of Health and Human Services and the Office of Management and Budget to develop the CDC budget for inclusion in the President’s budget request. In support of the President's budget request, when the President submits the Executive Branch Budget to Congress each year, the Executive agencies submit a document called the Congressional Justification. This document "justifies" the request to the President by explaining the mission of the agency, outlining the goals and objectives of the coming fiscal year, and provides comparative data for the previous, current, and proposed budget. Reports outlining CDC’s annual budget requests can be found at http://www.cdc.gov/budget/index.html.

What is the GDD program’s future direction?

As the program moves forward, we plan to:

  • Continue to develop core public health capacities in all GDD Centers
  • Increase coordinated multicenter scientific collaboration across GDD Centers to strengthen the global network
  • Increase the number of public health professionals trained by GDD programs
  • Broaden and strengthen global partnerships
  • Reduce gaps and build capacity in global preparedness for emerging health threats

GDD and Global Health Security

What is the GDD program’s role in global health security?

CDC has a long and storied history of supporting global health protection, ranging from smallpox eradication, cholera control, polio elimination, and emergencies including SARS, novel influenza (H5N1, H1N1) and, most recently, Ebola. The Global Disease Detection (GDD) program was initiated in the early 2000s to promote a broader approach to preparing countries for any infectious disease threat that could occur, regardless of pathogen.

Everything we do through the GDD program helps build country capacity in alignment with the International Health Regulations (IHR), which are an international framework agreed on by all WHO member countries to improve global health security. How does the GDD program’s work support the Global Health Security Agenda?

GDD was one of the first ways that CDC systematically approached global health security, and has provided the basis and lessons learned for our work on the Global Health Security Agenda. Six of the countries with GDD Centers have also been identified as Global Health Security Agenda countries (Bangladesh, India, Kenya, Kazakhstan, Thailand, Georgia).

The countries with GDD Centers have already made great strides in many of the foundational elements of building their public health systems, including:

  • Improved laboratory networks
  • Stronger surveillance systems
  • Workforce development
  • Established emergency operations centers

And these countries are better prepared to take on the other elements of the Global Health Security Agenda, including more focused work on zoonotic disease detection, antimicrobial resistance, and multisectoral collaboration. All of these elements will be accelerated in GDD countries because of the relationships, science, and infrastructure that have been strengthened through our GDD investments.

Additionally, countries that have been strengthened through GDD Centers will be more likely to serve as regional leaders, sharing their technical expertise with other countries as they promote global health security.

GDD Country Selection and Expansion

What goes into prioritizing where GDD Centers are placed?

The criteria below are used to determine where to put a GDD Center. These also are used more generally in decisions to support other new CDC global programs.

  • Public health significance: The country has a high population density or history of infectious diseases or expected potential for emerging diseases;
  • Country commitment: The country supports and values partnership with CDC and will actively engage in collaborative activities and identify new partners;
  • Established CDC presence: The country has an established, effective working relationship with CDC and supports CDC staff in-country;
  • Established regional reach: The country has the infrastructure and regional stature to serve as regional resource, or is already acting as a regional leader in other arenas;
  • International partner presence: The country has other U.S. Government agencies and international partners operating in-country.

Does existing U.S. program infrastructure overseas help determine the location of GDD Centers?

Established presence of CDC and other US Government agencies in a country has been a helpful factor in deciding where to place a GDD center. We want to ensure the success of GDD Centers in helping the countries where they are placed to improve their capacity to prepare for public health threats, and a history of strong relationships with the US government can be a good indicator of a willingness on behalf of the host government to work closely with us.

One example of working with existing infrastructure relates to the U.S. Department of Defense. CDC has had a strong relationship with DoD for many years, and our GDD Centers are good examples of this. Our GDD Centers often work closely with the Naval Medical Research Units (or NAMRUs) within their regions, and they are able to collaborate on information sharing and partnership building. For example, the Egypt GDD center works with public health institutions in partnership with the NAMRU-3 based in Cairo. The GDD Center utilizes NAMRU-3’s state-of-the-art laboratory facilities, including BSL-3 space, and builds on over 60 years of NAMRU-3’s successful regional public health partnerships.

Monitoring & Evaluation

How is the GDD program’s work evaluated?

Beginning in 2006, GDD began routinely monitoring the program’s capabilities and progress using a framework that includes quantitative and qualitative information related to the following five key activities:

  • Outbreak Response: Improving the timeliness and reliability of outbreak investigations and responses
  • Pathogen Discovery: Advancing public health knowledge through innovative research into the epidemiology and biology of emerging infections and identifying novel threats before they spread
  • Training: Building capacity and improving the quality of epidemiology and laboratory science through training
  • Surveillance: Strengthening surveillance systems that are capable of detecting, assessing, and monitoring the occurrence and public health significance of infectious disease threats over time
  • Networking: Enhancing collaboration through shared resources and synergy

More about our process can be found on our Data and Reports page.

Where does the information for program evaluation come from?

As part of the program, updating these measures is done through our in-country GDD Center. We have dedicated staff that spends a portion of their time on this activity. They work with our Atlanta-based staff to validate the information for accuracy. It is important to note that we are only measuring the aspects of the program accomplished through the GDD Center, and do not seek to assess host country capacity as a whole.

GDD Centers also maintain their own performance data which encompasses not only the data set required by GDD, but that of our other funding partners such as DoD, Department of State, USAID, and to fulfill their own, locally relevant data needs.

In the case of countries adopting their own criteria for success and measuring similar functions, we may work with countries on that, but it is not reflected in our reports. Country ability to fulfill their own, locally relevant data needs was the rationale behind the adoption of the GDD program and remains so today.
