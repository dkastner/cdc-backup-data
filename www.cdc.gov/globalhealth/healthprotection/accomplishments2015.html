2015 in Review: Global Health Protection in Action

Recommend on Facebook Tweet ShareCompartir

Global health protection is not about one country, one issue, or one pathogen. From detecting and responding to infectious disease outbreaks and humanitarian crises to preventing chronic illness and injury – strengthening global health protection saves lives. And no country stands alone. In today’s world of global travel and trade, we need to look out for one another, for the sake of us all.

CDC’s Division of Global Health Protection and its partners around the globe are working together to keep us all safer and healthier. Our work is nonstop and diverse: last year, we took 437 trips to 59 countries. We offered partnership and expertise; helped whenever and wherever humanitarian emergencies struck; tracked diseases and outbreaks; graduated new disease detectives; discovered new pathogens; and tackled noncommunicable diseases. No matter what the issue, our action always centers on people, science, systems, and response. Here are 10 ways we did it in 2015:

1. Learning by Doing: Building Better Systems in the Wake of Ebola

The issue: The Ebola epidemic brought the world’s attention to the need for all countries to be prepared for health threats before they happen. Widespread transmission of Ebola in West Africa has been controlled – but the work is not over. With thousands of survivors, there is still much to learn.

Taking action: Over the last year, CDC continued to respond to the largest Ebola outbreak in history, deploying 1,387 public health experts to the field. We worked side by side with countries to do what was needed, from laboratory testing to contact tracing to community education and support. In the midst of the outbreak, we stood up permanent field offices in Guinea, Liberia and Sierra Leone to bring public health leadership and resources closer to those who need them most. Surveillance systems and laboratory testing multiplied. Better tracking and data led to smarter decisions. A new legacy of disease detectives began – in Liberia, for example, the first cohort graduated from the 3-month Field Epidemiology Training Program; the 30 graduating members were selected from the Liberian Ministry of Health and have begun mentoring the next Liberian FETP cohort.

  • Discover the wider lessons we learned from the Ebola epidemic
  • See how the Ebola response shifted how we view Global Health Security
  • Learn why changing attitudes and behavior is critical in stopping the spread of disease

 

2. Ready, set, go: Implementing the Global Health Security Agenda

The issue: As outbreaks like SARS and Ebola have shown us, the world must put systems in place to stop infectious diseases before they cross borders. In 2015, we continued working with countries to reach the goals of the Global Health Security Agenda (GHSA).

The United States has committed to helping at least 30 countries achieve all of the targets of the Global Health Security Agenda by the end of 2019 and is investing more than $1 billion in resources.

Taking action: CDC is taking a lead in helping the U.S. meet its GHSA commitments. We are building on proven programs, like Global Disease Detection, to inform our way forward. Priorities in 2015 included working through established Global Disease Detection Centers to launch GHSA priorities in six countries; growing the global workforce by training field epidemiologists, developing new testing methodologies to strengthen laboratory systems, and putting the systems in place to recognize illness fast and close to the source. We also provided extensive support to 15 countries in completing their five-year “road maps” toward building a world safe from infectious disease.

  • About 70% of countries remain unprepared to respond to an outbreak. Learn how the Global Health Security Agenda aims to put systems in place for a safer world
  • See what CDC’s Division of Global Health Protection is doing every day to help countries prevent, detect, and respond to disease threats
  • Get answers to top questions about the Global Health Security Agenda and how it relates to the Global Disease Detection program

In case you missed it: The Neglected Dimension of Global Security: A Framework to Counter Infectious Disease Crises. Strengthening public health as the foundation of the health system is the first line of defense. Spending a few billion dollars a year globally to improve national health systems provides a small insurance policy against catastrophic risks.

3. No Time to Wait: Global Rapid Response Team Stands Up

The issue: When public health emergencies happen, trained experts must arrive on scene quickly and stay for as long as necessary.

Taking action: Last year, CDC established a Global Rapid Response Team (Global RRT) to answer the call. The team is prepared to respond to heath threats whenever and wherever they occur.

Global RRT supports international emergency responses ranging from large-scale disease outbreaks to natural disasters. Team members are on call, pre-trained, and ready to deploy across the globe, enabling CDC to respond rapidly to emergencies with larger numbers of CDC staff. In 2015, the team participated in five outbreak responses in four countries: Gabon, Indonesia, Sierra Leone, and Tanzania. This is in addition to a total of 143 deployments to 28 countries by our emergency response and recovery experts in 2015.

  • Learn why CDC's Global Rapid Response Team is about much more than outbreaks
  • Find out about CDC: Emergency Response and Recovery experts, who travel the world to help populations in need

 

4. Building a Workforce: Disease Detectives in the Field

The issue: Countries around the world need “boots on the ground” in the ongoing battle against infectious diseases, chronic diseases, and environmental hazards. Disease detectives are needed to gather information and turn it into public health action.

Taking action: By training disease detectives in their own countries, CDC’s Field Epidemiology Training Program aims to meet the global health security goal of establishing a well trained workforce, including at least one trained field epidemiologist per 200,000 people worldwide.

In addition to responding to Ebola in 2015, we developed and launched 18 new training courses, including a new front line curriculum. We saw the relaunch of the Francophone West Africa FETP in 7 countries and the launch of the new Advanced FETP in Uganda. The Advanced FETP in Saudi Arabia was relaunched and supported 12 investigations of Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS).

  • Explore stories about disease detectives around the world
  • Find out how the Uganda FETP got a crash course in disease detection
  • See a conversation with CDC Director Tom Frieden as he provides insight for an FETP resident in Indonesia

 

5. Better Data for Better Decisions: Data for Health

The issue: Globally, two-thirds (38 million) of 56 million annual deaths are not registered, and almost half of the world’s children go unregistered at birth. Without accurate information on births and deaths, health professionals and governments can’t effectively decide how to address the needs of their communities.

Taking action: In an innovative partnership, CDC is collaborating with Bloomberg Philanthropies, the CDC Foundation, and other partners to work toward strengthening information on births and deaths in low- to middle-income countries across Latin America, Asia, and Africa. CDC is working with countries to implement mobile phone surveys, improve communications, and build efficient systems to collect and share data. Countries will use improved public health information to make policy decisions and help 1.2 billion people across the globe live longer, healthier lives.

  • Take an in-depth look at Bloomberg’s Data for Health Initiative
  • Discover CDC’s priority activities in the Data for Health partnership
  • Check out CDC’s training Course on Civil Registration and Vital Statistics Systems

 

6. Hypertension: Controlling the “Silent Killer”

The issue: Studies show that excess sodium intake – too much salt added to food -- is a key risk factor for hypertension. Each year, hypertension, also known as high blood pressure, causes an estimated 10 million preventable deaths worldwide. A 2007 study found that reducing average sodium intake by 15% could prevent 8.5 million deaths over 10 years, and would only cost $0.05 per person.

Taking action: CDC’s work on hypertension expanded its reach to new countries in 2015 through the Global Standardized Hypertension Treatment Project (GHSTP). CDC is also working with China on the Shandong Province & Ministry of Health Action on Salt and Hypertension (SMASH) project. SMASH aims to change the way salt is used, bringing prevention into homes and restaurants by providing scaled spoons for home cooking, holding low-salt recipe contests, and training chefs.

  • Learn how CDC is working to reduce hypertension and save lives
  • Explore a webinar, slides, audio resources, and more in our Global Hypertension Toolkit
  • Discover how CDC’s noncommunicable disease program is building capacity to address hypertension globally

 

7. Preventing Outbreaks in Humanitarian Crises

The issue: Diseases like cholera have the potential to wreak havoc among the world’s most vulnerable populations. Limited access to safe water and adequate sanitation makes it easy for cholera bacteria to infect and kill large numbers of people in a very short time. As we learned in the aftermath of the 2010 Haiti earthquake, camps for refugees and internally displaced persons can be particularly susceptible to outbreaks.

Taking action: As CDC’s emergency response and recovery experts respond to humanitarian crises around the globe, they remain vigilant against diseases that can spread in temporary camps. Ongoing work in countries like Iraq, Kenya, and Tanzania has showed us the value of close surveillance and education in internally displaced and refugee populations.

 

  • See the six areas we focus on to improve the world’s access to safe water, sanitation and hygiene
  • Get an update on our progress with cholera in Haiti

 

Did You Know? Between March 2014 and December 2015, the GDD Operations Center tracked over 196 outbreaks in 127 countries, in addition to Ebola. From MERS in the Middle East to cholera in East Africa and chikungunya in the Americas, these outbreaks justify global concern and have affected many lives.

 

8. Antibiotic Resistance: The Global Threat

The issue: If nothing is done to stop the rise of antimicrobial resistance, we may soon lose our power to treat even common diseases. Despite this frightening reality, cultural roadblocks can sometimes stand in the way of addressing the danger: people misusing antibiotics; laboratories lacking equipment to identify the source of disease; physicians lacking trust in lab results or technology; and taboos against collecting and sharing personal health data.

Taking action: CDC’s Global Disease Detection program has joined with partners around the world to bring down these roadblocks, working closely with hospitals and communities to put systems in place to improve diagnosis and change the way antibiotics are used.

 

  • Check out our easy-to-understand infographic on the threat of antimicrobial resistance
  • Learn why addressing antimicrobial resistance is a global imperative
  • See how our team in Egypt is protecting the power of antibiotics

 

9. A Commitment to Africa’s Health

The issue: “The West African Ebola epidemic reaffirmed the need for a public health institute to support African ministries of health and other health agencies in their efforts to prevent, detect, and respond to any disease outbreak.” - CDC Director Tom Frieden.

Taking action: In 2015, a Memorandum of Cooperation formalized collaboration between the African Union Commission and the U.S. CDC to create the African Centres for Disease Control and Prevention (African CDC). Says Dr. Frieden, “This memorandum solidifies the commitment by the United States to advance public health across Africa and global health security.”

The African CDC joins other National Public Health Institutes, like the U.S. CDC, in protecting the health of people across the globe. Our NPHI program partners with the International Association of National Public Health Institutes (IANPHI), whose 100 members reach five billion people across four continents.

  • See how CDC’s National Public Health Institutes program is providing expertise across the globe
  • Learn why National Public Health Institutes are critical to public health
  • Get more information on the African Union CDC partnership to launch an African CDC

 

10: A 15-Year “To-do List” for Global Health

The issue: In 2015, all 193 UN Member States adopted 17 Sustainable Development Goals that cover social, environmental, and economic targets. “The Sustainable Development Goals are our shared vision of humanity and a social contract between the world's leaders and the people,” said UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon. “They are a to-do list for people and planet, and a blueprint for success.”

Taking action: In 2016, the world must come together to make progress toward these goals. CDC will play a significant role in Sustainable Development Goal number 3, which is a call to “ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages.”

We address many of the targets under this goal, including noncommunicable disease targets and strengthening the capacity of all countries, in particular developing countries, for early warning, risk reduction and management of national and global health risks.

Keep up with our news and discoveries in 2016 as we work to help people across the globe live longer, healthier lives.
